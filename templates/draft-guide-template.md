## How to use this template

1. **Fill it out during your planning session.** Read your source material, discuss with Claude, make decisions, then output this document.

2. **Do not draft prose in the planning session.** The DraftGuide is the deliverable. Prose happens in a fresh chat.

3. **Start the drafting session by loading this.** Tell Claude: "Read this DraftGuide. This is the plan. Draft the chapter."

4. **The beat sequence is a guide, not a script.** Beats can merge, reorder, or compress during drafting. The locked decisions are the things that don't move.

### When to add a spine step

If your chapter has more than three candidate scenes and you're not sure which ones carry the chapter versus which ones are texture, add a spine step before filling out this template. Ask Claude to propose three or four candidate spines, each naming what the chapter is really about, which scenes carry it, and what compresses to texture. Pick one, then build the DraftGuide around it.

Without the spine step, Claude tends to give every scene equal weight, producing a chapter that covers everything and isn't about anything.

## Real Example: DraftGuide from *Too Many Goats on the Mountain*

This is a condensed version of the actual DraftGuide used for a chapter about the author leaving a company after thirteen years. You can give this entire template, including this example, to Claude and say: "Here's what another project did. Help me build mine."

### Header (as written)

**Chapter 11: "Stick a Fork in Me Because I'm Done"**
**Target length:** ~2,800 words (ceiling)
**Voice register:** Quiet. Coming to a close, not building toward something.

### What this chapter is about

The end of thirteen years at one company. Multiple threads converging: the stock crash aftermath, a friend's death, and the decision to leave. Not a rage quit. An ending that had been building for years.

### Beat sequence (condensed)

1. **The IPO floor experience** — NYSE trading floor, the Cramer appearance, the 9/11 memorial nearby. The spectacle. Son watching on TV at home.
2. **The stock crash** — Specific date, specific numbers. Analyst downgrades. The bottom. What the economics actually looked like after taxes.
3. **The friend** — A phone call. Two sentences of actual dialogue. The breakdown afterward. A hike on a mountain. Tight, no backstory expansion.
4. **The departure** — Told the CEO he'd do a three-month transition. Picked an end date. Started looking.
5. **Closing line** — "Onward." One word. Attributed to an early colleague who always said it after hard losses.

### Locked decisions

- No specific share counts. Keep economics directional.
- The friend's section is tight. No expansion into backstory. Two sentences of dialogue, then the emotional beat.
- A major financial payoff (the company's later acquisition) moved to the epilogue. Not in this chapter.
- The chapter's leanness is intentional. Shortest chapter in the book. Endings don't need to be long.

### Material held out

- The transition to the next company (job search, interviews, competing offers) deferred to Chapter 12.
- The company's later acquisition deferred to the epilogue.

### What makes this example work

The "what this chapter is about" section is one paragraph, not a list of events. The beat sequence says what each beat *does for the chapter*, not just what happens. The locked decisions prevent the drafting session from re-litigating things the planning session already resolved. And the "material held out" section explicitly names what is NOT in this chapter, which is just as important as what is.
