## How to use this template

1. **Fill in what you know now.** Voice, hard rules, and a few editorial principles. Don't try to complete everything before you start writing.

2. **Add to it as you go.** The best style guide rules come from catching problems in actual drafts. When you fix something, add the rule that prevents it from happening again.

3. **Load it every session.** Tell Claude to read this before doing anything else. It's the single source of truth.

4. **Version it.** Save as `[ProjectName]_StyleGuide_v1.md`, increment the version number every time you update it.

### Questions to ask yourself before your first chapter

- What does my narrator sound like? (Record yourself telling one of your stories. That's the voice.)
- What should this book absolutely NOT sound like?
- Are there any punctuation or formatting rules I feel strongly about?
- How do I want to handle real people in the book?
- What's my profanity policy?
- Am I the hero of every scene, or something else?

## Real Example: Style Guide from *Too Many Goats on the Mountain*

This is a condensed version of the actual style guide used across 34 chapters of a first-person Silicon Valley memoir. You can give this entire template, including this example, to Claude and say: "Here's what another project did. Help me build mine."

### Voice (as written)

**Target register:** Rene telling a story to a smart friend in a bar.

First person, past tense. Scenes built around characters with agency carry the chapter. Humor from the situation, not from construction. Show rather than tell. Trust the reader.

The narrator is an active participant and observer, not the hero of every scene. Other characters drive scenes they should be driving.

**What this voice is NOT:** Not polished corporate memoir. Not startup advice book. Not "lessons from a leader." Not a writer constructing scenes at a desk.

### Hard Rules (as written)

**No em dashes in prose.** Em dashes are a Claude AI tell. Use commas, periods, or restructure. The entire 80,000-word book has exactly 3 em dashes in the text, each one explicitly approved.

**No colons in narrative prose sentences.** Same tier as the em dash rule.

**No semicolons in narrative prose.**

**Banned constructions:** "And that's when I learned..." / "Looking back..." / "Journey" as a metaphor / "Learnings" / "Buckle up" / "I realized that..." / Meta-commentary that explains the lesson of an anecdote / Thesis restatements at the end of a chapter / "Genuinely," "honestly," "straightforward" (Claude-isms).

### Editorial Principles (selected)

1. **Show, don't tell. Cut meta-commentary.** If a paragraph starts explaining what the previous paragraph meant, cut it.
2. **Authentic company language preserved, generic metaphors cut.** Test: did someone actually say this in the hallway?
3. **Preserve the funny lines.** The humor IS the voice. Don't trim jokes for pace.
4. **Superlatives earn their place.** "Hardest decision of my career" was cut from Ch16 because harder decisions come later.
5. **Author-voice aphorisms get cut.** If a sentence sounds like something an author would write rather than something the narrator would say, it goes.

### What makes this example work

The voice description is one sentence, specific and visual ("a smart friend in a bar"). The "what this voice is NOT" list prevents Claude from drifting into adjacent genres. The hard rules are absolute, not suggestions, because Claude treats suggestions as optional. The editorial principles all trace back to specific problems caught in actual drafts. And the banned constructions list grew over the project as new AI tells were discovered.
